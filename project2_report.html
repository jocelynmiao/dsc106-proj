Proposition: "Developing Economies emit much more CO2 than Developed Economies"

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Transformation: We converted raw average emission values into percentage change relative to 2010, using 0 as the baseline. Setting the baseline to 0 makes it easier for readers to seperate increases and decreases by using positive and negative numbers. This transformation can be misleading, however, as readers may assume that carbon emissions have strictly increased each year for developing economies and strictly decreased each year for advanced economies, despite potential fluctuations between each year.
  • Color Choice: We decided to use red for developing and emerging economies and green for advanced economies. Originally, we used green for the emerging/developing economies and gray for the other because we wanted to draw more attention to the group with larger emissions. However, we realized that green, along with a trend of increased values, could contribute to a positive perception, despite greater CO2 emissions being more harmful. We wanted to create a stricter divide between "good" and "bad" between the emissions of the two groups, which is why emerging/developing economies are red and advanced economies are green. For similar reasons, we highlighted the baseline to green, creating positive associations for values below the line.
  • Type of plot: We went with a box plot. Partially to differentiate from the negative propostion, but mostly to highlight general trend rather than year-by-year breakdowns. By reducing overall noise we are able to showcase overall behavior of each group. Additionally, this sends a very clear message to the reader: that developing and emerging markets have strictly positive distributions and advanced markets have strictly negative distributions in percentage change relative to 2010.

Final Reflection

The design process of these visualizations consisted of playing with the data around our proposition that "developed" economies produce less CO2 emissions than "developing" economies. Though vague, we interpreted the terms "developing" and "developed" as an aggregation of countries or locations where their respective economic infrastructure is still blooming or already heavily invested in. Whether these variables were aggregations in the dataset, it was straightforward for us to construct non-deceptive visualizations that supported the proposition. However, it proved more difficult to find a way to construct an opposing visualization. The method to twist the interpretation of the data to counter the same proposition required some level of deceptiveness, but maliciousness would not be the intent. Had we actually apply a transformation that changed the interpretation - first, it would have been very likely the proposition could have changed.

While transformations may change the interpretation of a visualization, it is important to recognize that such transformations are not automatically "unethical". The ethics in an analysis and visualization likely require the analyst to explicitly state the trasnformations applied and the possible other interpretations with the same data - for the analysis to be "ethical." An analysis that doesn't do this and confidently forwards its interpretation leans towards being unethical - transforming data without explicitly noting said transformation AND not considering alternative interpretations may appear malicious in intent. We have arrived at this interpretation because it is very possible to unintentionally arrive at a visualization that may feel deceptive. Therefore, to dispel any possible interpretations of being intentionally unethical, it's important to address the design process and decisions.

Admittedly, the bounds between acceptable and misleading persuasive choices are difficult to draw. "Acceptable" persuasive decisions may include being "misleading." Succeeding in persuading may inherently "mislead" the audience to believe an interpretation, but does this success make the choice "acceptable?" Of the possible boundaries that can be drawn, an acceptable persuasive choice would be one that does not require much rationale to justify the decision, while a misleading choice would be something that might be more difficult for readers to understand at a glance. For example, a transformation of changing units or using percentage change instead of raw values - should count as an acceptable persuasive choice because little was done to the original data and readers shouldn't be confused by such a decision. Meanwhile, applying a multitude of transformations may confuse readers, because interpretability becomes muddied and many hurdles have to be jumped to get to the conclusion. In these circumstances, these could be considered more concrete boundaries - that can be drawn. Persuasive choices that fall between these extremes become difficult to classify as "acceptable" or "misleading." In any case, providing rationale helps justify the persuasive decisions of the designer rather than leaving the audience to try decipher the intentions behind the visualization.
